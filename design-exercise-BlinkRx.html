The brief · where I started 02 / 09
Reading the brief

The brief combined multiple systems into one app.

The requirements included

  • ordering
  • reservations
  • social interaction
  • workplace coordination
  • operational forecasting
At first glance, this looked like a cafeteria ordering app.

But the more I unpacked the brief, the more it felt like a coordination problem between people, space, and operations.

That made me pause and ask: what's the actual underlying problem here?

Questions I sat with 03 / 09
Mapping the tensions

Before scoping, I wrote down every question worth asking.

Grouped into five clusters. Most of these won't get answered in this kickoff — but writing them down made the tradeoffs visible and forced me to commit to a point of view.

01Ordering & flexibility
  • Should employees dine in, take out, or both?
  • How far in advance should orders happen?
  • Should instant / walk-in orders still be allowed?
  • How does flexibility hurt forecasting accuracy?
02Seating & coordination
  • Booking solo or as a group?
  • Is lunch partially a space problem?
  • What happens if you order food but can't find a seat?
This cluster kept pulling me back. The seating question turns out to be the hinge.
03Social features
  • Why would employees socialize inside a cafeteria app?
  • What kind of social interaction feels natural in a workplace?
  • Would anyone actually want another feed?

A workplace utility isn't a place people want to hang out socially.

04Personalization
  • Allergies — must be visible, not buried
  • Dietary preferences as filters
  • Repeat-order behavior
  • Efficiency over discovery — this is a utility
05Menu & operations
  • Fixed menu, dynamic menu, or hybrid?
  • Stable core + rotating specials?
  • Planned vs instant orders — forecast tradeoff
  • Reducing food waste
The insight that changed scope 04 / 09
What I noticed

Nothing in this product works in isolation

Across every cluster, the same theme kept surfacing. Ordering depends on seating. Seating depends on attendance. Attendance depends on coordination. Forecasting depends on all of it.

Key insight

The challenge is not food ordering. It's coordinating people, lunch, seating, and cafeteria operations in a hybrid workplace.

What this reframe unlocks

Once I treated lunch as a coordination problem, the product structure became clearer. The kitchen's forecasting problem and the employee's social problem stop being separate features — they're the same flow seen from two angles. The reservation is the forecasting signal.

The reframe

A cafeteria ordering app

Product principles 05 / 09
Rules used to say no

Principles guiding tradeoffs

These are the heuristics I used to cut features and choose between options.

PRINCIPLE 01

Support social flexibility, not forced participation.

Some people want lunch alone. That's a valid mode, not a failure state. The product shouldn't pressure people into group behavior.

PRINCIPLE 02

Coordination over social networking.

"Social" is interpreted as lightweight coordination, not feeds, posts, or chat. Workplaces already have Slack/Teams/Email (but they can share infomation to Slack/Teams/Email).

PRINCIPLE 03

Planning, not scheduling.

Encourage intent ("I'll be in Tuesday, want to grab lunch?") without enforcing rigid calendars. Lunch is a soft commitment.

PRINCIPLE 04

Solo and group are equal citizens.

The architecture treats individual orders inside a group reservation the same as standalone orders. No second-class flows.

A note on "social". I read the brief carefully. "Socialize" doesn't have to mean a feed. In a workplace, the most natural social interaction at lunch is just knowing who's around and being able to invite them to a table. That's coordination, not a network.

Experience modes · scope decision 06 / 09
Going deep, not wide

Defined 4 product modes.

For a 2-hour design exercise, I chose to go deep on the mode that combines the most tensions.

01 Group lunch Reserve a table, invite teammates, everyone orders their own meal inside the reservation. The mode that combines ordering, reservations, hybrid coordination, lightweight social, and forecasting. Today's focus
02 Solo pickup Order ahead, grab and go. Eat at desk or back to a meeting. Later
03 Solo dine-in Order, reserve a seat for one. Quiet lunch, optionally at an "open table". Later
04 Walk-in / instant Show up, order at counter. Lower forecast signal but preserves flexibility. Later

In scope for kickoff

  • Group lunch coordination flow
  • Table reservations tied to a group
  • Individual ordering inside a group reservation
  • Lightweight invitations
  • Dietary filters & allergy markers
  • Repeat-order shortcut
  • How the signal feeds kitchen forecasting

Intentionally out

  • Solo flows (share data model, designed later)
  • Social feed, posting, commenting
  • Persistent chat or DMs
  • Payment & checkout details
  • Admin / kitchen-side UI
  • Onboarding
  • Notifications surface

Why group lunch: it's the only mode that naturally combines all five tensions from the brief. The solo modes are simpler variants of the same data model, designing the hard one first means the others mostly fall out.

Core experience - Mia's Tuesday.

The journey of one organizer and her team on a day they're all in the office. I sketch the story before the screens so the screens have something to serve.

2

4

5

The forecasting signal

By the time Ana confirms her order in step 4, the kitchen already knows three meals are landing at 12:15 at table 4. Multiply that across 200 reservations and you have a demand curve, not a guess.

Where I'd go next 09 / 09
Closing the kickoff

What I'd want to validate before going further.

If this were day 2

I'd validate the reframe with 4–5 employees and 1–2 cafeteria managers before adding fidelity. Specifically:

  • Do people actually want to coordinate lunch, or do they just want to know who's in?
  • How sticky is the "table reservation" mental model in an office context?
  • Does the kitchen trust pre-order signals enough to actually pre-prep?
  • Is the "open table" feature a delight or a privacy problem?
Open questions I'm sitting with
01 Is "group lunch" the right primary, or am I optimizing for the most differentiated mode rather than the most common one?
02 How does this interact with calendar tools people already use?
03 What's the cost of being wrong about forecasting for the kitchen, and for trust in the app itself?
